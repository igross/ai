Fertility, Baby Booms, and the Political Economy of Housing Supply
Draft for project 03 (extension of the NIMBY housing-supply model)
Date: March 1, 2026

ABSTRACT
This paper develops a fertility extension of the NIMBY housing-supply framework from project 02.
The baseline project-02 mechanism links local political preferences to endogenous housing supply
tightness. The extension adds endogenous fertility and a lagged cohort-political channel:
a temporary baby-boom shock can increase later political resistance to housing supply, raise
future housing prices, and reduce later fertility. We present side-by-side simulations against
an old-model proxy that excludes the fertility channel, and we clarify exactly what the current
prototype can and cannot establish. The model is a mechanism exercise and does not yet identify
causal effects in US data. We close with an empirical implementation plan for a US fertility
panel with explicit immigration/cohort accounting.

1) INTRODUCTION
The main question is whether demographic shocks can feed back into housing politics and then
affect fertility in the next generation. In the original NIMBY model, housing supply outcomes are
determined by political equilibrium and demand pressures. In this extension, fertility and
demographics are part of the state transition.

Conceptual sequence:
1. A baby-boom shock temporarily raises births.
2. As these cohorts age into politically relevant groups, local supply preferences tighten.
3. Tighter supply raises housing costs and can lower subsequent fertility.

This paper is separate from project 02. It reuses the political housing-supply core, but asks a
distinct question and introduces new dynamic channels.

2) RELATION TO THE NIMBY PAPER
Inherited from project 02:
- political housing-supply block
- endogenous housing-price dynamics

New in project 03:
- endogenous fertility choice
- lagged baby-boom cohort entry into the political block

3) MODEL
State variables:
- M_t(j): population share in age bin j
- p_t: housing price index
- theta_t: political supply tightness
- b_t: births
- f_t: fertility rate
- n_home_t: lagged births proxy for children-at-home demand pressure

Fertility (new channel):
- Desired fertility:
  f*_t = clip( f_level * exp(-eta_p * p_t), 0.05, 0.85 )
- Observed births with temporary baby-boom multiplier mu_t:
  b_t = f*_t * sum_{j in F} M_t(j) * mu_t
  mu_t = 1 + boom_amp on [t_start, t_end], else 1

Political tightness:
theta_t = clip(
  theta_0
  + theta_old * old_t
  - theta_young * young_t
  + theta_boom * average_lagged_births,
  0, 0.95
)

Housing block:
- Demand:
  D_t = d_0 + d_young * young_t + d_birth * b_t + 0.15 * n_home_t
- Supply:
  S_t = s_0 + eps_0 * (1 - theta_t) * exp(-p_t)
- Price update:
  p_{t+1} = (1-lambda) * p_t + lambda * [ p_t + g * (D_t - S_t) ]

4) SIMULATION DESIGN
- Horizon T = 80
- Damping lambda = 0.25
- Baby-boom shock: +60% births from t=15 to t=24
- Post-shock evaluation window starts at t=40

Compared models:
- New model: endogenous fertility + lagged boom-to-politics channel
- Old proxy: pre-extension logic without endogenous fertility response

6) WHAT THIS DOES AND DOES NOT SHOW
What it shows:
- The extension is internally consistent with the mechanism:
  baby-boom cohorts can tighten later housing politics and reduce later fertility,
  conditional on calibration.

What it does not show:
- No causal empirical estimate yet for US fertility decline.
- Not yet the full project-02 steady-state MATLAB stack in this repo copy
  (upstream .mat dependencies missing).
- Immigration and migration composition effects are not yet estimated.

7) EMPIRICAL IMPLEMENTATION: US FERTILITY PANEL
Immediate objective:
Test whether the model-implied dynamic pattern appears in US data once migration/immigration
composition is handled explicitly.

Panel target:
- Geography-time: county-year or MSA-year panel (state-year fallback)
- Outcomes: age-specific fertility rates, first-birth timing proxies, cohort-completed fertility proxies
- Housing: permits, completions/unit mix, price/rent indices, reform timing
- Demographics: migration flows and foreign-born share changes by age cohort

Identification:
1. Staggered event study around housing-supply reforms.
2. Political close-election design where feasible.
3. Geographic-supply IV as complementary margin.

Baseline estimators will include immigration/cohort decomposition terms so fertility changes are
not conflated with compositional shifts.

8) CONCLUSION
This is a separate paper built on the NIMBY framework with one core extension:
endogenous fertility and lagged demographic-political feedback. The current model delivers
a plausible mechanism in which temporary baby booms can worsen later housing-supply politics
and reduce subsequent fertility. The next stage is empirical discipline using a US panel with
explicit immigration-aware cohort accounting.
