# 02 Literature review and synthesis

## 1. Scope
This review focuses on evidence relevant to learning by viewing: when video-based instruction improves skill acquisition, through which mechanisms, and under what implementation conditions. The objective is to build a usable synthesis for model design and empirical identification rather than a long catalog of disconnected studies.

## 2. Causal evidence from school settings
The strongest identification evidence comes from randomized or quasi-randomized interventions in school systems. Banerjee et al. (2007) show that computer-assisted learning in India improved test outcomes when instruction was better aligned to student level. Muralidharan, Singh, and Ganimian (2019) find large gains from technology-aided instruction in an RCT setting, with especially meaningful effects where content adapts to learner needs. Escueta et al. (2017) synthesize this broader literature and emphasize that effect sizes are highly context-dependent and mediated by implementation quality.

The key lesson for this project is that raw access is not the treatment of interest. Effective exposure, content quality, and learner-task fit are the mechanisms that plausibly drive gains. This implies we should estimate interaction effects and mechanism margins, not only average treatment effects.

## 3. Tutoring as benchmark and complement
Tutoring evidence gives a practical benchmark for what high-touch instructional support can achieve. Bloom (1984) frames the classic one-to-one benchmark, and Nickow, Oreopoulos, and Quan (2020) show large average effects in modern experimental evidence. For this project, tutoring matters for two reasons. First, it sets a realistic upper benchmark for short-run skill gains. Second, it clarifies where viewing may complement rather than substitute for human support.

A useful empirical framing is to estimate whether exposure effects are larger when support intensity is high in complex or risky tasks, and smaller where tasks are repetitive and low risk. This avoids overclaiming that one modality dominates universally.

## 4. Online and blended learning in higher education
Higher-education evidence separates learning effects from access effects. Figlio, Rush, and Yin (2013) show modality can affect learning even in tightly controlled settings. Joyce et al. (2015) show classroom time still matters in core economics courses. Goodman, Melkers, and Pallais (2019) highlight that online delivery can improve access while leaving performance effects heterogeneous.

This strand is directly useful because the project should measure both margins: extensive margin outcomes (participation, completion, persistence) and intensive margin outcomes (scores, pass rates, error rates). A null on one margin does not imply irrelevance on the other.

## 5. Platform design, video quality, and learner behavior
MOOC and platform studies clarify that treatment intensity is shaped by content design and platform rules. Guo, Kim, and Rubin (2014) show that video format and production choices correlate strongly with engagement. Kizilcec, Piech, and Schneider (2013) document distinct engagement trajectories across learner types. Reich (2015) cautions that much early MOOC inference lacked strong counterfactuals and overgeneralized from selected populations.

For this project, this evidence motivates quality-adjusted exposure measures and careful separation of access from realized usage. Platform data can be very informative, but causal interpretation requires exogenous variation in access, ranking, or rollout.

## 6. Cognitive foundations from multimedia learning
Cognitive theory provides the micro-foundation for heterogeneous effects by task type. Mayer and Moreno (2003) and Mayer (2005) show why dual-channel processing and limited working memory can make visual demonstration effective for representationally complex tasks. Ginns (2005) and Hoffler and Leutner (2007) find modality and animation effects that are positive on average in some contexts but clearly moderated by design quality and task demands.

The practical implication is to avoid binary treatment definitions when possible. Exposure should be adjusted for quality signals such as segmentation, pacing, clarity, and demonstrability.

## 7. Task-based economics as organizing frame
Task-based economics provides a clean structure for interpretation. Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003) and Acemoglu and Autor (2011) show that technology effects are task-specific rather than uniform. Acemoglu and Restrepo (2019) emphasize displacement and reinstatement margins in task space, which can be adapted to instructional technology: viewing may increase productivity in tasks where procedural information is hard to convey by text alone.

This motivates a core empirical object: the interaction between exposure and procedural intensity. It also motivates theory that links modality choice, task attributes, and dynamic skill accumulation in one framework.

## 8. Infrastructure and access as identification margins
Broadband and access studies are useful for design, but interpretation is subtle. Akerman, Gaarder, and Mogstad (2015) show broadband is skill-complementary in labor outcomes. Malamud and Pop-Eleches (2011), Fairlie and Robinson (2013), and Vigdor, Ladd, and Martinez (2014) show that home technology access does not automatically produce achievement gains without productive usage.

For this project, infrastructure variation can provide credible quasi-experimental timing, but the preferred interpretation should remain exposure-based rather than access-only.

## 9. Synthesis for this project
The literature supports a focused baseline: estimate differential exposure effects by procedural intensity, then test mechanism channels through quality and support interactions, then evaluate persistence over time. This sequence keeps claims aligned with available evidence and avoids overreliance on weakly identified platform correlations.

## 10. Open gaps this project can fill
- Joint estimation of task heterogeneity, quality-adjusted exposure, and dynamic persistence in one framework.
- Clear complement-versus-substitute evidence between viewing and human support.
- Evidence that is credible on identification and usable for policy choices on instructional design and access investments.
