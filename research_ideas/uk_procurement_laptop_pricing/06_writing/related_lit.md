# related literature positioning

This paper sits at the intersection of public procurement, competition design, and measurement of price dispersion in institutional purchasing.

## public procurement and competition
A central literature studies how entry, bidder counts, auction/procedure format, and transparency rules affect procurement outcomes. Our project connects to this work by using bidder participation and route indicators (framework call-off, direct award, open procedure) as mechanism variables for conditional price gaps in a standardized-goods setting.

## waste, incentives, and organizational frictions
Another strand distinguishes active versus passive waste and studies how procurement institutions convert governance frictions into spending outcomes. Our design contributes by separating observed price differences into bundle-content explanations versus residual gaps potentially linked to competition/compliance frictions.

## incomplete contracts and renegotiation
Incomplete-contract theories and renegotiation evidence are often most salient for complex infrastructure or bespoke service projects. Laptops provide a useful contrast: they are standardized products with external price observability. If meaningful residual gaps remain after strict comparability controls, those gaps are less likely to be explained by project-specific complexity alone.

## transaction costs of compliance and governance
Compliance requirements can reduce risk and improve accountability while increasing transaction costs or slowing purchase cycles. Our timing and route analyses are designed to test whether those frictions map into measurable price wedges in a commodity IT context.

Relative to existing work, this project emphasizes transparent SKU-date benchmarking, explicit bundle comparability flags, and route-level mechanism tests within UK institutional structures.
