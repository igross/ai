# introduction draft

Public procurement of standardised technology goods creates a basic policy puzzle. On one hand, public buyers in aggregate are large purchasers, often buying repeated products at scale under formal contracting structures. Standard textbook intuition suggests that scale, repeated interaction, and central procurement frameworks should allow the public sector to obtain favorable prices. On the other hand, descriptive discussions in policy and practice often suggest that public purchasers can appear to pay more than private-sector benchmarks for similar goods. Both claims can be true depending on what is being compared. A line in an award schedule that appears to be "a laptop" may include service, compliance, and deployment obligations that are not embedded in a retail shelf price. The practical challenge is therefore not only to estimate a price gap, but to define a credible like-for-like comparator and to separate mechanisms that can generate observed differences.

This paper studies that challenge in the UK context, focusing on laptops as a deliberately hard test for claims of public-sector overpayment. Laptops are tradable, heavily standardised, and usually described with SKU-level characteristics that can be matched across procurement records and market sources. Relative to complex bespoke projects, laptops should minimize ambiguity from design risk and post-contract renegotiation. If substantial residual price gaps remain even in tightly matched, hardware-only laptop transactions, that is informative. If gaps largely disappear after accounting for warranty, support, imaging, security, and delivery commitments, that is equally informative. The empirical contribution is to make this decomposition explicit rather than treat all observed differences as waste or all differences as justified quality.

The core research question is: do UK public-sector buyers pay higher per-unit prices for comparable laptop SKUs than contemporaneous market benchmarks, after accounting for VAT treatment, bundle content, and procurement mechanism? We evaluate four mechanism hypotheses. First, bundling can explain apparent overpayment when contract lines combine hardware with services. Second, reduced effective competition under certain framework or direct-award routes can raise markups. Third, compliance requirements and procurement cycle duration can create timing and transaction-cost wedges relative to rapidly moving market prices. Fourth, threshold-driven procedure changes can alter competition and compliance intensity in ways that affect prices near policy-relevant cutoffs.

The empirical design is built around an observation-level panel indexed by SKU, contract, award date, and buyer. On the public side, we collect award notices and attachments from UK procurement portals, with particular attention to records where unit pricing is recoverable from pricing schedules. On the market side, we construct contemporaneous benchmark prices using a pre-specified hierarchy: manufacturer list prices where feasible, enterprise channel prices where available, and major UK retail prices as fallback. For each matched observation, we retain explicit comparability metadata (warranty alignment, service content alignment, date distance, and confidence score), so the main estimand can be defined on a conservative sample rather than inferred from noisy text alone.

The baseline outcome is a log price gap: the difference between the log public unit price and the log benchmark market price for the same SKU and date window. This metric supports intuitive interpretation and straightforward decomposition. We begin with descriptive evidence on the distribution of gaps in a high-credibility sample: high-confidence SKU matches, explicit quantities, net-of-VAT harmonization, and hardware-only or separable line items. We then estimate mechanism regressions linking price gaps to procurement route variables, including framework indicators, direct-award indicators, and bidder-count proxies, while controlling for SKU fixed effects, time fixed effects, and buyer (or buyer-type) heterogeneity. These models are explicitly interpreted as conditional associations unless stronger identifying variation is established.

To move beyond correlation where feasible, the paper proposes a threshold-based quasi-experimental design around procurement rule discontinuities that may alter procedure and transparency requirements. Contracts near relevant thresholds can, in principle, provide local comparison groups with similar underlying demand conditions but different procedural obligations. However, this design is included with strict feasibility conditions: sufficient density near cutoffs, no strong evidence of manipulation in the running variable, and continuity of covariates. If those diagnostics fail, threshold evidence will be presented as descriptive rather than causal. This conditional approach avoids overstating identification strength while preserving policy-relevant structure in the analysis.

Several confounds are first-order and are handled as design constraints rather than post hoc caveats. The largest is bundling. If hardware and services are jointly priced and inseparable, comparison to hardware-only market prices is not valid for the main overpayment estimand; those observations are analysed in a separate bundled category. The second is timing mismatch: laptop prices can move quickly as product cycles evolve, so benchmark windows around award dates are pre-specified and tested for sensitivity. The third is VAT treatment and commercial terms (delivery, warranty, support), which are harmonized or flagged for exclusion in baseline specifications when uncertain. The fourth is selection into procurement route: urgent or complex purchases may choose routes correlated with price outcomes. Fixed-effects structures and sample restrictions can reduce but not eliminate this issue, so interpretation remains disciplined.

This project contributes in three ways. First, it provides a transparent measurement framework for public-versus-market price comparison in a context where unit-price data are scattered across semi-structured attachments rather than clean transaction databases. Second, it offers mechanism evidence that separates bundle content from competition and governance channels, rather than collapsing all differences into a single "efficiency" narrative. Third, it adds UK-specific evidence on procurement design for standardised IT goods, an area where policy choices around frameworks, call-off methods, and disclosure standards have immediate budget implications.

The expected policy value is practical. If measured gaps are concentrated in bundled contracts, policy should focus on standardizing and disclosing service components rather than only pursuing lower headline hardware prices. If gaps persist in hardware-only comparisons and are systematically linked to low-competition routes, interventions might target call-off design, bidder participation, and direct-award governance. If timing frictions dominate, process-speed reforms and more dynamic benchmark referencing may be more effective than route redesign alone. In each case, the key is to identify where the wedge originates.

The rest of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 provides UK institutional background on portals, frameworks, and route mechanics. Section 3 details data construction, attachment parsing, SKU matching, and benchmark hierarchy. Section 4 presents identification and main specifications, including threshold design requirements. Section 5 reports descriptive and regression results, with explicit decomposition by bundle status. Section 6 presents robustness and falsification tests. Section 7 discusses policy implications, limitations, and external validity.
