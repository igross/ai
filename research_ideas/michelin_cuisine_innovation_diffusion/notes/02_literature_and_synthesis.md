# 02 Literature and synthesis

Last updated: 2026-02-26

---

## Purpose

Build a paper-ready literature base for a project on culinary innovation, diffusion, and homogenization in Michelin-star markets. The goal is synthesis for design choices, not a broad catalogue.

---

## Diffusion and social learning

Griliches (1957) and Bass (1969) establish adoption dynamics where uncertainty and information accumulation generate gradual takeoff and saturation. Foster and Rosenzweig (1995) and Conley and Udry (2010) show that peer learning can materially shift adoption timing in high-uncertainty environments. Banerjee et al. (2013) show network structure matters for speed and reach of diffusion.

Design implication: treat each culinary idea as an adoption process with hazard shaped by network exposure.

## Networks and creative spillovers

Bala and Goyal (1998) formalize neighbor-based learning. Bramoulle, Djebbari, and Fortin (2009) provide identification logic for peer effects in network data. Jaffe, Trajtenberg, and Henderson (1993) show localized spillovers. Uzzi and Spiro (2005) show creative outcomes depend on network topology, especially cohesion plus brokerage.

Design implication: build directed, time-varying chef networks from career paths and collaboration links rather than static co-location.

## Status markets and conformity pressures

Rosen (1981) and Krueger (2005) motivate concentrated returns to rank and attention. Podolny (1993) and Benjamin and Podolny (1999) show status as a market signal. Phillips and Zuckerman (2001) and Hsu (2006) show conformity and category legibility pressures in ranked fields.

Design implication: model novelty choice under status incentives and test nonlinear responses across status tiers.

## Haute cuisine and Michelin-specific institutional dynamics

Ferguson (1998) frames gastronomy as a cultural field. Rao, Monin, and Durand (2003, 2005) document identity movements and category boundary change in French cuisine. Svejenova, Mazza, and Planellas (2007) show elite chefs as institutional entrepreneurs. Ottenbacher and Harrington (2007) and Aubke (2013) provide Michelin-adjacent innovation and network evidence. Sands (2025) studies strategic reactivity to Michelin recognition.

Design implication: combine institutional narrative with econometric identification around chef-move and star-transition events.

## Attention, reviews, and visibility shocks

Chevalier and Mayzlin (2006), Anderson and Magruder (2012), and Mayzlin, Dover, and Chevalier (2014) show online information environments shape demand and strategy. Luca and Zervas (2016) and Berger and Milkman (2012) support attention-based mechanisms and strategic response to visibility.

Design implication: use attention shocks as exogenous or plausibly exogenous shifters of imitation returns.

## Measurement: text and image as data

Gentzkow, Kelly, and Taddy (2019) and Grimmer and Stewart (2013) define best practices for text-as-data inference. Blei, Ng, and Jordan (2003) and Kozlowski, Taddy, and Evans (2019) provide representation approaches. Hoberg and Phillips (2016) operationalize text-based differentiation in economics. Radford et al. (2021) provides modern image-text representation tools.

Design implication: define transparent novelty and homogenization indices from text/image embeddings and validate against hand coding.

## Synthesis for this project

The most coherent first-paper design combines:
- a network-diffusion core (mechanism identification),
- a status-incentive layer (innovation vs conformity decisions), and
- a measurement framework that turns menu/image content into estimable state variables.

This supports a narrow first contribution: identify how network and status channels shape diffusion speed and resulting style convergence.
