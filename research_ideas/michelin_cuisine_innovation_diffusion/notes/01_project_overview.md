# 01 Project overview

Last updated: 2026-02-26

---

## Objective

Build a long-run culture-and-innovation economics project in which elite culinary style is an endogenous state variable shaped by networks, status incentives, and media attention.

## Motivation

Michelin-star cuisine is a useful laboratory for studying idea diffusion because innovation is visible, high-stakes, and repeatedly evaluated. Kitchens experiment under strong incentives to differentiate while preserving evaluator legibility. This creates a natural tension between creativity and conformity, with potential long-run style convergence.

Most economic work on innovation diffusion focuses on technology and firms outside cultural production. Most sociology and hospitality work on haute cuisine is rich descriptively but less connected to modern causal identification and structural measurement. This project combines both.

## Core research questions

1. How do culinary ideas emerge and diffuse across Michelin restaurants over time?
2. Which channels matter most: chef networks, imitation, and attention shocks?
3. Does diffusion produce homogenization in menu and visual style, and where does diversity persist?
4. How does Michelin status change innovation incentives before and after recognition?
5. Can we connect micro style dynamics to long-run cultural-development questions?

## Conceptual framing

Style is treated as a vector of observable culinary features from menu text and dish images. Restaurants choose innovation intensity given costs, expected demand, and evaluator incentives. Diffusion operates through lineage and collaboration networks plus global visibility shocks. Homogenization appears when common motifs diffuse broadly and local idiosyncrasies weaken.

## Scope for this phase

In scope:
- Build a credible first paper design with one primary mechanism and one strong complementary mechanism.
- Construct a feasible public-data-first pipeline (Michelin metadata, chef histories, menus, archived pages).
- Define measurable novelty and homogenization indices with validation plan.

Out of scope:
- Full structural estimation of all channels in the first draft.
- Heavy dependence on restricted social-platform data.
- Global harmonization beyond a manageable pilot geography.

## Data backbone (initial)

- Michelin metadata: restaurant identity, stars, location, cuisine, year.
- Chef network data: biographies, career moves, lineage links, collaborations.
- Menu corpus: restaurant sites and archived snapshots.
- Image corpus: legally accessible restaurant gallery material and archived images.
- Attention proxies: search trends and major food-media coverage.

## Working definitions

- Innovation: introduction of low-prevalence textual/visual culinary motifs relative to contemporaneous peers.
- Diffusion: increase in adoption probability for an idea after exposure through network or attention channels.
- Homogenization: increase in pairwise style similarity and/or decline in city-level style entropy.
- Style vector: fused representation from menu text and image embeddings.

## Baseline hypotheses

- H1: Higher exposure to adopter peers in chef networks increases idea adoption hazard.
- H2: Attention shocks accelerate diffusion, especially for visually salient ideas.
- H3: Michelin recognition changes post-event novelty choices (direction to be tested).
- H4: Global diffusion raises cross-restaurant similarity unless strong lineage identity offsets it.
- H5: Mid-status restaurants display stronger conformity than frontier or low-status restaurants.
