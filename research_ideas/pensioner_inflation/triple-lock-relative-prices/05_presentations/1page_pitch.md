# one-page pitch

## question
Does UK state pension uprating under the triple lock raise relative prices in pensioner-intensive local sectors via demand-composition effects?

## hypothesis
H1: Positive pension purchasing-power shocks increase demand for pensioner-intensive non-tradables, and where local supply is inelastic this raises relative prices. A secondary channel may operate through financing instruments (e.g., NIC-like payroll margins) that raise marginal costs in labour-intensive services.

## proposed method
1. Build a national pension shock series from uprating data relative to CPI/earnings benchmarks.
2. Use regional retiree exposure in a shift-share panel framework for category-region inflation outcomes.
3. Construct a pensioner-weighted inflation basket from LCF weights and compare to headline inflation.
4. Use policy-episode event windows (where applicable) with retiree density as treatment intensity.
5. Stress-test with placebo categories, pre-trends, and alternative shock definitions.

## data
- ONS CPI/CPIH category inflation and services-goods splits.
- ONS LCF age-specific expenditure shares.
- ONS regional population age structure.
- DWP/ONS series for state pension levels and uprating history.
- Sectoral labour/capacity proxies for local non-tradable supply elasticity.

## contribution
- Integrates demographics, transfer indexation, and local relative-price formation in one UK-focused framework.
- Provides practical measurement of pensioner-basket inflation and its wedge vs headline inflation.
- Delivers policy-relevant evidence on whether indexation design has composition effects beyond aggregate transfer incidence.
