# outline

## 1. introduction
- Research question: does triple-lock uprating shift relative prices via pensioner demand composition?
- Why this matters: ageing economies, politically indexed transfers, inflation heterogeneity.
- Main hypotheses and empirical predictions.

## 2. institutional background: UK triple lock as political indexation
- Rule mechanics and timeline.
- Episodes of deviation/suspension and why they matter for identification.
- Fiscal context and financing margins.

## 3. mechanism framework
- Two-group, two-sector model intuition.
- Demand-composition channel.
- Financing and marginal-cost channel.
- Testable propositions and falsification conditions.

## 4. data
- CPI/CPIH category inflation.
- LCF age-specific expenditure weights.
- Pension uprating and macro controls.
- Regional retiree exposure and supply proxies.

## 5. empirical strategy
- Shift-share regional-category design.
- Pensioner basket inflation differential design.
- Event-style regional DiD around policy regime changes.
- Identification assumptions and diagnostics.

## 6. planned results
- Baseline estimates for pensioner-intensive non-tradables.
- Exposure heterogeneity by retiree density.
- Cost-channel extension with labour intensity/payroll proxy interactions.

## 7. robustness and falsification
- Placebo tradables and low-exposure categories.
- Alternative pension shock measures.
- Alternative exposure definitions and trend controls.
- Sensitivity to inflation index choice (CPI vs CPIH variants).

## 8. welfare and distribution discussion
- Incidence across workers and pensioners.
- Renters vs owners and regional disparities.
- Policy trade-offs for indexation design.

## 9. conclusion
- Mechanism summary.
- Evidence interpretation boundaries.
- Implications for ageing economies and transfer indexation policy.
