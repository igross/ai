# introduction draft

Inflation is usually discussed as a macroeconomic aggregate, yet households do not face a single inflation rate. They face basket-specific price growth that depends on what they buy. In ageing societies, this distinction becomes more than a measurement footnote. If older households have systematically different expenditure patterns, then policies that alter pensioner incomes may not only change distribution; they may also change relative prices through demand composition. This paper asks whether that mechanism is visible in the United Kingdom under the state pension triple lock.

The UK case is analytically useful because the triple lock is an explicit, salient, and politically durable indexation rule. In broad terms, the rule links state pension uprating to high-level macro indicators rather than to local sectoral conditions. That feature creates a national time-varying pension income process that can diverge from headline inflation and from wage growth. The central hypothesis of this project is that such uprating can behave like a politically determined pseudo-wage for pensioners: when pension purchasing power rises, spending pressure increases in categories that pensioners consume relatively intensively, especially in locally supplied services. If supply in those sectors is inelastic in the short run, relative prices can rise.

The primary mechanism is demand composition. Pensioners and workers allocate spending differently across tradables and non-tradables. The project assumes, as a testable empirical claim, that pensioners devote larger shares to selected local service categories than working-age households do. When a transfer shock raises pensioner income, aggregate demand reweights toward those categories. Under elastic tradable supply, tradable prices adjust little locally. Under inelastic non-tradable supply, local service prices can absorb more of the adjustment. The model therefore predicts stronger effects in pensioner-intensive categories and in retiree-dense areas, with the largest responses where local capacity is tight.

A secondary mechanism is financing incidence. If pension uprating is effectively financed in ways that raise labour costs, for example through payroll-tax-like margins, there may be an additional cost-push channel in labour-intensive services. That channel is conceptually distinct from demand composition. In reduced form, both can raise service inflation, but they imply different cross-sectional signatures. Demand composition should scale with retiree exposure and age-specific basket intensity. Cost-push effects should scale more directly with labour intensity and financing instruments, even conditional on retiree exposure. A useful contribution of this project is to put both channels in one framework and design tests that can partially separate them.

The empirical setting requires caution. National inflation episodes over the past decade include large common shocks, notably in energy, food, and broad wage dynamics. A time-series relationship between pension uprating and aggregate inflation would therefore be uninformative. Identification must instead rely on interaction structure: national pension shocks combined with regional exposure and category exposure. The baseline design follows this logic with a shift-share style specification. Regions are assigned exposure based on retiree concentration (and, where feasible, pension-income share). National pension shock measures capture variation in pension purchasing power, defined as actual uprating relative to inflation, wage growth, or forecast-based expectations. Outcomes are category-region inflation rates, with emphasis on services and non-tradables.

The paper also proposes a complementary measurement design at the national level: a pensioner-weighted inflation index built from age-specific expenditure weights and category inflation series. The key object is the wedge between pensioner-basket inflation and headline inflation. The project tests whether this wedge co-moves with pension shock measures in ways consistent with the model. This approach is not causal by itself, but it disciplines mechanism claims and informs category selection for the regional design.

A third design layer uses policy-episode variation. Triple-lock implementation has included periods of adjustment, suspension, or methodological debate. Where discrete timing can be documented, a regional difference-in-differences/event-style setup can use retiree density as treatment intensity. Because policy timing is national, this strategy does not rely on policy variation across places; it relies on differential exposure to the same national rule change. Power may be limited by the number of events, so this design is framed as supportive rather than standalone.

The project contributes in three ways. First, it offers a compact mechanism linking politically indexed transfers to relative prices through demographic demand composition and local supply elasticity. Second, it proposes a practical measurement strategy that combines pensioner-basket inflation construction with category-region panel outcomes. Third, it develops an identification framework tailored to UK institutions, where policy timing is national but demographic exposure is highly uneven across space.

Several threats are central and will be treated as first-order design constraints. Retiree-dense regions differ systematically in amenities, migration patterns, and sector mix; these factors can correlate with inflation independently of pensions. Local supply shocks, including labour shortages or weather-related disruptions in selected sectors, can contaminate estimates. Category mapping is imperfect: some pensioner-intensive expenditures may not align neatly with CPI categories, and some categories have national pricing. To address these risks, the project uses multiple outcome bundles, explicit placebo categories (particularly tradables), pre-trend diagnostics, and interactions with supply proxies. Claims will be limited to patterns that survive these stress tests.

The paper is intentionally forward-looking and does not pre-judge empirical magnitudes. The mechanism could be economically modest, concentrated in a narrow set of categories, or absent once confounders are controlled for. Those are informative outcomes. A null result in retiree-exposure gradients, especially in low-elasticity service categories, would materially weaken the core hypothesis. Conversely, consistent exposure-gradient results across independent measurement approaches would support the view that transfer indexation rules can shape inflation composition, not merely transfer levels.

The broader relevance extends beyond the UK. Many advanced economies combine population ageing with politically salient transfer systems. If indexation rules shift relative demand in ways that interact with local supply frictions, then inflation policy and transfer policy are more connected than standard aggregate frameworks imply. The UK triple lock provides a transparent institutional laboratory for testing that possibility.

This project therefore proceeds with a disciplined sequence: establish the institutional timeline and shock measurement, document expenditure-share heterogeneity by age, estimate exposure-based category effects, run falsification exercises, and only then interpret welfare implications. The intended output is not a broad claim that pensions drive inflation, but a narrower and testable claim: under specific demographic and supply conditions, pension uprating can move relative prices in pensioner-intensive local sectors.
