# 01 Project overview

Last updated: 2026-02-25

---

## Objective

Build a long-run political economy and growth research programme in which linguistic systems
(accents, phonetic traits, dialect structure) are endogenous historical state variables that
both shape and respond to integration, state capacity, and development.

## Motivation

Most economics papers treat language as fixed and coarse (common-language dummy, linguistic
distance at country level). Historical linguistics shows that speech systems evolve through
transmission, contact, and institutional pressure. This project integrates those dynamics
with economic history to answer two joint questions:

1. How did political and economic shocks change local speech over decades and centuries?
2. Did those speech changes matter for market integration, migration, and development?

## Central research questions

1. **Persistence:** why do dialect regions persist despite migration and state standardisation?
2. **Change:** what drives gradual sound change versus rapid transitions (chain shifts)?
3. **Convergence vs divergence:** when do urbanisation and migration homogenise speech, and
   when do they generate durable differentiation?
4. **Economic relevance:** do accent/dialect differences measurably affect exchange, matching,
   and diffusion?
5. **Political economy:** how do schooling, media, and nation-building reshape local speech?

## Conceptual framing

Language is modelled as:
- a communication technology that affects transaction and matching costs;
- an identity marker with prestige and status content;
- a culturally transmitted trait with path dependence and noise.

Political economy forces enter as transport/market-access shocks, migration-induced network
rewiring, schooling and curriculum standardisation, broadcasting/media exposure, and
administrative capacity expansion.

## Scope for this phase

**In scope:** UK-first data architecture and one lead empirical MVP. Theory section with one
or two compact dynamic models and testable comparative statics. Historical depth from early
modern to modern where data allow.

**Out of scope:** full structural estimation across all mechanisms; multi-country
harmonisation before UK MVP succeeds; deep phonetics pipeline requiring restricted audio
licences in first pass.

## Data backbone

- **Linguistic:** Survey of English Dialects / Linguistic Atlas of England; historical text
  corpora (EEBO/ECCO proxies); modern spoken corpora and archival audio (BL/BBC where
  feasible).
- **Economic and political:** historical census and migration data; railway GIS and
  market-access construction; media signal coverage and schooling reform exposure; regional
  labour outcomes and integration proxies.

## Working definitions

- **Accent:** pronunciation profile associated with speaker group or region.
- **Dialect:** broader bundle including phonology, lexicon, and syntax.
- **Phonetic innovation:** measurable shift in pronunciation target or proxy.
- **Convergence:** declining linguistic distance between areas or cohorts.
- **Levelling:** reduction in marked local variants due to contact or standard pressure.

## Hypotheses

- H1: Earlier rail integration increases dialect convergence in treated districts.
- H2: Greater dialect distance lowers bilateral migration and market integration.
- H3: Media rollout accelerates convergence toward prestige variants.
- H4: Schooling standardisation reduces cohort-level local phonetic divergence.
- H5: Linguistic convergence mediates part of the rail-to-market-integration effect.
