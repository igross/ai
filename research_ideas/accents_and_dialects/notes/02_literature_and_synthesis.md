# 02 Literature and synthesis

Last updated: 2026-02-25

---

## Purpose

Build a usable literature base for a long-run political economy paper where linguistic
systems evolve over time and interact with economic development, state formation, migration,
and integration. Organised for paper construction, not as a catalogue.

---

## Economics: language and dialect as economic friction

Falck, Heblich, Lameli, and Suedekum (2012) show that regions with historically similar
dialects exchange more migrants, using 19th-century German dialect geography matched to
modern flows. Lameli, Suedekum, Nitsch, and Wolf (2015) extend this to intra-German trade.
Melitz (2008) and Melitz and Toubal (2014) decompose the language effect in international
gravity into spoken-language, translation, and common-origin channels. Adsera and Pytlikova
(2015) find linguistic proximity strongly predicts international migration.

These papers establish that dialect distance has real economic content. The gap is that all
treat language as static. This project adds dynamics.

## Economics: culture, distance, and diffusion

Spolaore and Wacziarg (2009) show historical and cultural distance predicts slower diffusion
of development. Desmet, Ortuno-Ortin, and Wacziarg (2017) distinguish linguistic from
cultural distance and show they are correlated but not equivalent. The empirical strategy
must separate communication friction from identity channels.

## Policy, identity, and language standardisation

Clots-Figueras and Masella (2013) show language-policy exposure shifts identity outcomes.
Fouka (2020) finds coercive assimilation can backfire and sustain minority language. Bandiera,
Mohnen, Rasul, and Viarengo (2019) show compulsory schooling functioned as integration
technology during mass migration. Alesina, Reich, and Riboni (2017) provide a formal
nation-building framework with state incentives for language standardisation.

## UK bridge paper

Fouka and Serlin (2024) use industrialisation and migration to explain cultural identity
change in 19th-century Britain — the closest direct template for our UK historical design.
The extension is to replace name-based identity proxies with dialect/phonetic measures.

## Economic history: integration shocks

Donaldson and Hornbeck (2016) estimate large market-access gains from US railroads. Donaldson
(2018) provides the Indian counterpart. Gregory and Marti-Henneberg (2016) offer UK-specific
GIS evidence on rail and local demographic change 1825-1911 — direct infrastructure for our
treatment coding. Dittmar (2011) shows the printing press caused higher city growth, a clean
historical communication-technology precedent. Adena et al. (2015) show radio signal
variation had large political effects, supporting BBC-based language-convergence designs.

## Historical linguistics foundations

Weinreich, Labov, and Herzog (1968) define the core explanatory problems for language change.
Labov (1994) documents systematic chain-shift patterns. Blevins (2004) shows sound patterns
emerge from articulatory biases and transmission. Pierrehumbert (2001) shows exemplar storage
and frequency generate directional sound change. Griffiths and Kalish (2007) and Kirby,
Cornish, and Smith (2008) formalise iterated learning dynamics. Baxter, Blythe, Croft, and
McKane (2006) model variant competition with slow dynamics and transitions. Harrington,
Palethorpe, and Watson (2000) show acoustic drift is measurable over decades in elite speech.
Newberry, Ahern, Clark, and Plotkin (2017) develop tools to separate drift from selection.

## Synthesis for this project

The literature supports a design where: (a) transport/media/schooling shocks provide
treatment variation; (b) dialect/phonetic measures capture linguistic outcomes; (c) gravity
frameworks test economic consequences of dialect distance; (d) dynamic models with
coordination and exemplar mechanisms generate testable comparative statics.
