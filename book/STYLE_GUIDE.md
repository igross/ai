# The Target Trap — Style Guide

Based on a close reading of *How to Read Numbers* (David & Tom Chivers, 2021), *Everything is Predictable* (Tom Chivers, 2024), and *The AI Does Not Hate You* (Tom Chivers, 2019).

---

## The combined voice

*The Target Trap* is co-authored, which means it should feel like *How to Read Numbers* did: a single unified "we" voice that combines David's economist's precision with Tom's journalistic warmth. The best version of this voice is:

- Accessible and friendly, but never dumbed down
- Gently self-deprecating — the authors are not above the problems they describe
- Wry rather than laugh-out-loud funny
- British in idiom and cultural reference
- Structurally careful (economist's instinct) but narratively alive (journalist's instinct)
- Willing to say "we don't know" or "this is complicated" rather than forcing false clarity

The enemy is: the textbook wearing a cardigan. The goal is: the excellent pub conversation with two very well-read people who happen to have a framework for explaining everything.

---

## How chapters are built

### The opening hook
Every chapter should open with something concrete, relatable, and slightly wrong — a policy, a headline, a rule, an incident where someone tried to fix a problem and made it worse. The hook should make the reader think "yes, I know exactly what you mean" before they know where you're going.

**Established hooks we've used:**
- Ch 1: The dirty dishes rota. Introduced to solve a problem, immediately makes it worse, creates a new row about the rota itself. *"The human race has eradicated smallpox; it has travelled to the moon; it has peered into the heart of the atom. But we still cannot get people to wash their own bloody dishes."*
- Ch 2: The offside rule. Introduced to stop goal-hanging. Immediately gamed. VAR introduced to enforce it precisely. Makes the game worse.

### The concept introduction
Introduce the concept by name early, but don't define it until you've shown what it does. Goodhart's Law appears by name about two pages into Ch 1 but the definition comes after a worked example of schools and GCSE grades. The order is: *feeling* → *name* → *definition*.

### The worked example
Every chapter needs at least one example worked through in enough detail that you feel the mechanism, not just recognise the pattern. Small numbers. Round numbers. Named fictional people if needed. This is the economist's instinct — show the moving parts.

**Established examples we've used:**
- GCSE A*–C target: schools focus on D→C borderline pupils, ignore top and bottom. This exact mechanism was observed in British schools. *(Ch 1 — don't repeat, just reference)*
- Hospital mortality targets: hospitals refuse very sick patients. *(Ch 1 — signposted; developed properly in Ch 5)*
- Scientists publishing as many papers as possible because citations = worth. *(Ch 1 — signposted; developed properly in Ch 7)*
- Partner sent for groceries, returns with wine, Minstrels, and lawn seed (it was half price). *(Ch 1 — the lighthearted principal-agent illustration)*
- Offside trap: defenders step up just before the pass, leaving attacker offside. Notts County 1920s. Arsenal 1990s. Liverpool high line. *(Ch 2)*
- VAR: accuracy rises from 92% to 98%. But fans half-celebrate, half-wait. *(Ch 2)*
- NFL catch rule: Dez Bryant 2014 no-catch, Calvin Johnson 2010. Each rule rewrite creates new grey areas. *(Ch 2)*
- D&D edition wars: rules rewritten to close exploits, creating incompatible parallel worlds. *(Ch 2)*

### Wider applications
After the main worked example, two or three further cases extend the concept — one accessible/amusing, one more consequential. This builds the reader's confidence that the pattern is real and widespread, not a cherry-picked curiosity.

### The close
Chapters should close with a brief synthetic observation, not a summary. Not "so we have seen that..." — instead, a short observation that reframes what you've just read slightly, or plants a forward connection. The close of a *How to Read Numbers* chapter often has the form of a crisp "rule for readers." The Target Trap can do the equivalent: a short crystallisation of what this chapter's lesson means for how to think.

## How to introduce concepts to non-economists

### Name things
Use the proper names — Goodhart's Law, Campbell's Law, the Cobra Effect, the McNamara Fallacy, the principal-agent problem, time inconsistency, moral hazard. These names do real work: they tell the reader "this is a recognised pattern, not just a thing that happened once." They also give readers something to google and take away.

### Give the plain-English version first
The formal definition comes *after* the intuition has been established. We do not open with "the principal-agent problem is any situation where one person acts on behalf of another with imperfect information alignment." We open with the kitchen rota.

### Use the toy example
When you need arithmetic, use small numbers. The *How to Read Numbers* method: "imagine 100,000 people." The Target Trap equivalent: "imagine five people in a department." Make it concrete enough to count on your fingers.

### The optional box
For genuinely technical content that some readers will want but others won't — use the *How to Read Numbers* device of the set-off optional box: *"You don't need to read this, but if you'd like to go deeper..."* This solves the dual-audience problem without patronising either group.

### Absolute numbers alongside percentages
Always. "A 6% improvement" means nothing without knowing the denominator. This is a *How to Read Numbers* rule worth carrying into this book.

## Rhetorical devices that work

### The self-deprecating confessional
Both books begin with some version of: "we are not above this problem ourselves." In *How to Read Numbers*: "Tom thinks he is pretty bad at maths." In the Target Trap, we should establish early that targets and metrics catch everyone — including economists who study them.

### The unexpected example that broadens it
After the obvious example (schools, hospitals), one unexpected example that shows the pattern operates somewhere the reader would never have thought to look. In Ch 2 we already used D&D edition wars as the unexpected one. This kind of sideways move is important — it's what makes the reader think "this really is everywhere."

### The Postecoglou rule
Direct quotes from practitioners who articulate the gaming problem from the inside are valuable and memorable. Ange Postecoglou: *"I'll tell you what managers do, me included, we try to find ways to bend the rules. Tell me what the rule is, and I guarantee you'll have a room full of managers processing 'how can I get around this.'"* Find the equivalent for each chapter.

### The pop culture shorthand
A brief reference to something in shared culture (a TV show, a sport, a film) that illuminates the concept without labouring it. Tom's instinct; use it.

### The footnote
Footnotes are for: (a) the optional technical box, (b) a joke that would disrupt the main prose, (c) an honest caveat about a claim that's slightly stronger than the evidence strictly warrants. Do not footnote things that belong in the text.

### The callback
Plant something in one chapter, pay it off in another. The kitchen rota is planted in Ch 1 and should return in the Conclusion. The offside rule's "meta" concept in Ch 2 should echo in Ch 15 (AI specification gaming). These callbacks give the book a sense of architecture and reward attentive readers.

## The epigraph tradition

Both *Everything is Predictable* and *The AI Does Not Hate You* open with literary/philosophical epigraphs that do real intellectual work — they're not decorative. Ch 1 has the Saint Paul verse ("the letter killeth, but the spirit giveth life") which is perfect. Other chapters should follow suit where a good one exists, but not force it.

---

## What the book is NOT

- Not a policy paper (we diagnose; we don't prescribe legislation)
- Not an economics textbook (we use the concepts; we don't derive them)
- Not cynical (the book should leave the reader feeling equipped, not despairing)
- Not self-congratulatory about economic expertise (David is the economist; the book is for everyone)
