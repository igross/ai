\section{Literature Review}

This paper contributes at the intersection of three literatures:
necessity versus opportunity entrepreneurship, entrepreneurship under
financial frictions, and equilibrium labor-market search.

The first relevant strand is empirical work that distinguishes entrepreneurial
entry by labor-market origin and by business-cycle conditions. \citet{fairlie_fossen_2019} [CHECK]
show that transitions into entrepreneurship differ systematically between
those entering from non-employment and those entering from employment.
\citet{sedlacek_sterk_2017} [CHECK] document that startup cohorts are
cyclically heterogeneous in quality and growth outcomes. These results
motivate our focus on composition, not just the aggregate entrepreneurship
rate.

The second strand studies occupational choice and firm dynamics under
financial constraints. \citet{buera_2009} [CHECK] shows that credit
frictions affect both selection into entrepreneurship and post-entry
scale choices, with quantitatively important aggregate implications.
\citet{hurst_pugsley_2011} [CHECK] emphasize that a large share of
small-business activity reflects non-innovation motives, reinforcing
the need to model entrepreneurship as a broad margin rather than a
narrow innovation margin.

The third strand is the search-and-matching tradition in labor economics.
\citet{petrongolo_pissarides_2001} [CHECK] provide the benchmark synthesis
for job-finding and separation dynamics that underpins our labor-market
block and transition structure.

Relative to existing work, our contribution is to integrate these channels
in a single heterogeneous-agent framework with explicit state-contingent
occupational choice. This allows us to quantify how unemployment risk
and social insurance reshape the mix of necessity and opportunity entrepreneurship,
firm scale, and aggregate outcomes.
